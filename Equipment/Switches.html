Switches

Switches are used in LANs to allow electronic devices to communicate to each other within a network. Before I start explaining how switches work, I will explain what a MAC address is.

MAC Address

A MAC address is a hexadecimal address that is assigned to the device at the factory. Each device has a unique MAC address. An example of what a MAC address looks like: ED:2A:03:1F:58:AA. The MAC address is used to distinguish one device from another and is used in a variety of different ways, especially in switches. Unlike IP addresses that can change from hour to hour or day to day, MAC addresses don't change. Your device keeps the same MAC address forever.

How Switches Work

Kind of like how a router has a routing table of networks, switches has a somewhat similar table called the MAC Table. In this MAC table, it consists of MAC addresses and which port the device with that MAC address is connected to. This helps the switch forward ethernet frames easier because it knows where that device is. However, if you had just powered on your switch, the switch's MAC table will be blank. Even if you plugged 10 devices into the switch, the table will still be blank until traffic starts traveling through the switch. When traffic begins coming in, the switch will have to flood the frame out all ports because it doesn't know where the destination MAC address is.

There are 3 types of switching forwarding:

  1. Filter - The frame will get filtered if the destination is on the same port of the sender.
  2. Forward - The switch will forward the frame out of one port and into another port.
  3. Flood - The frame will get sent out of all ports (flooded) because the switch doesn't have the destination MAC address in its MAC table.

Switches build the MAC table by learning from the destination address of the ethernet frames that pass through it. When the switch doesn't know which port the destination MAC address is connected to, it will record the source address and which port the frame came from and then flood the frame out all ports. Only the device with that MAC address will respond; everyone else will discard the packet. As they are filtering, forwarding, and flooding frames, they build the MAC address. Once the switch has the MAC address of every device connected to it and which port they are connected to, the switch will no longer flood frames because it will already know where to send the frame out of. All switches really do is allow devices to communicate to each other within a LAN. They forward traffic out the correct port and allow the frame to traverse the LAN until it reaches the destination host.

If the switch receives a broadcast or multicast frame (the MAC address in the destination address field will contain all F's which will look like this: FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF), just like it would with an unknown destination MAC address frame, the switch will flood the frame out all ports to get the frame where it needs to go.
